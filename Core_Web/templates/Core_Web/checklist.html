Pre-Calving

Calving And Transition Checklist For Smallholder Farms

Use this checklist before the expected calving window begins. Tick each item off early so the farm is not improvising around dry-off, late pregnancy, or the first hours after birth.

Facilities
Calving pen cleaned and fully disinfected
Fresh deep bedding (straw) in place
Lighting checked for night calving
Handling area accessible nearby
Vet contact number posted clearly
Records And Reproductive Planning
Expected calving dates recorded per cow
Last insemination or breeding records updated
Dry-off dates planned about 45-60 days before calving
Vet and service-provider contacts saved and visible
Transition Cow Readiness
Pre-calving vaccinations completed on time
Dry cow nutrition programme in place
Feed and clean water access protected for close-up cows
Body condition scored at dry-off and at calving
Colostrum And Feeding
Frozen colostrum supply in freezer and labeled clearly
Colostrum replacer in stock as emergency backup
Clean bottles, nipples, and buckets ready
If available, a Brix refractometer or colostrometer is ready
Immediate Newborn Care
Airway clearing and drying plan ready
Navel dip prepared before birth
First colostrum target understood by the team
Calf standing and suckling monitored closely

Calving Toolkit

Stock these supplies before calving season. A professional setup reduces delay, contamination, and panic when a difficult case appears.

1
OB Lubricant
Essential before any manual intervention. Adequate lubrication reduces trauma to both the cow and the calf during assisted delivery.
2
Obstetric Chains And Handles
Store clean and ready. Apply correctly above and below the fetlock and avoid forceful pulling without proper assessment.
3
Clean Bucket And Disinfectant
Use chlorhexidine or betadine for disinfecting hands and equipment before any examination or assistance.
4
Esophageal Tube Feeder
For calves that cannot suckle promptly. Practice the technique before calving season begins, not in the middle of an emergency.
5
Thermometer
Normal calf temperature is roughly 38-39.5 C. Both hypothermia and fever require prompt action.
6
Iodine Solution
Prepare it ahead of time for navel dipping at birth and again at 24 hours to reduce early infection risk.

Quick Answers

Checklist Questions Farmers Ask Most

These are short reminders for the moment when you need quick answers, not the full training material.

Why should I record the dry-off date? ▼
The dry-off date helps the farm protect udder recovery, calf growth, and the next lactation. Without a recorded date, cows are easier to dry off too early, too late, or inconsistently.
When should the pen be ready? ▼
Prepare the calving area before the expected due window begins. The goal is to avoid cleaning, searching for bedding, or moving cows at the last minute.
What should be checked first after birth? ▼
Breathing, warmth, navel care, and colostrum timing should be checked first. Those early steps have the biggest effect on calf survival.
How much colostrum matters most? ▼
The first feed is the priority. High-quality colostrum should be given as early as possible, ideally within 2 hours of birth.
When should I stop waiting and call for help? ▼
If labor stalls, the presentation looks abnormal, or the calf appears compromised, stop relying on the checklist alone and move to the guide or veterinarian immediately.
